# Topology

$X$: a set, $\tau$: a collection of subsets of $X$.

Then, $\tau$ is a topology on a set $X$ when

1) $\emptyset \in \tau$, $X \in \tau$.
2) $\bigcup_{E \in \mathcal{V}} E \in \tau$ for every subfamily $\mathcal{V}$ of $\tau$.
3) $\bigcap_{E \in \mathcal{S}} E \in \tau$ for every finite subfamily $\mathcal{S}$ of $\tau$.

# Base

## Definition

$(X, \tau)$: topological space. Then, $\mathcal{B} \subseteq \tau$ is a base for the topology $\tau$ if

For all $E \in \tau$, there is a subfamily $\mathcal{S} \subseteq \mathcal{B}$ such that 
$E = \bigcup_{S \in \mathcal{S}} S$

## Properties

1) $\bigcup_{B \in \mathcal B} B = X$.
2) Let $B_1 \in \mathcal{B}, B_2 \in \mathcal{B}$, and $x \in B_1 \cap B_2$. 
Then, $\exists B_3 \in \mathcal{B}$ such that $x \in B_3$ and $B_3 \subseteq B_1 \cap B_2$.

#### proof.

1) Since $X \in \tau$, there exists a subfamily $\mathcal H \subseteq \mathcal{B}$ whose union is $X$.
By definition $B \subseteq X$ for all $B \in \mathcal{B}$. <br>
$\therefore X = \bigcup_{B \in \mathcal H} B \subseteq \bigcup_{B \in \mathcal{B}} B \subseteq X$.
2) Note that $B_1 \cap B_2 \in \tau$. By the definition of a base, $B_1 \cap B_2$ is the union of some subfamily of $\mathcal B$. <br>
Thus, for any $x \in B_1 \cap B_2$, there exists $B_3 \in \mathcal{B}$ such that $x \in B_3 \subseteq B_1 \cap B_2$.

# Topology generated by $ \mathcal{B} $

Suppose $X$ is just a set without any topology and $\mathcal B$ is a family of subsets of $X$
satisfying properties (1) and (2). And define $\tau$ as the family of all subsets of $X$
that are unions of subfamilies of $\mathcal B$. Then $\tau$ is a topology on $X$ and 
$\mathcal B$ is a base for $\tau$.

#### proof.

1) $X \in \tau$ by (1), $\emptyset \in \tau$ is trivial.
2) Suppose $\mathcal V \subseteq \tau$. Then each set $V \in \mathcal V$ is the union of some subfamily of $\mathcal B$. Let $S_V \subseteq \mathcal B$ be such subfamily; that is, $\bigcup_{E \in S_V} E = V$ for all $V \in \mathcal V$. It follows that: $$
\bigcup_{V \in \mathcal V} V = \bigcup_{V \in \mathcal V} \left( \bigcup_{E \in S_V} E \right)$$
Since the right-hand side is a union of elements from $\mathcal{B}$, the total union $\bigcup_{V \in \mathcal{V}} V$ must also belong to $\tau$. Therefore, $\tau$ is closed under arbitrary unions.
3) Suppose $\mathcal V_1 \in \tau$, $\mathcal V_2 \in \tau$. Then $\mathcal V_1$ and $\mathcal V_2$ is a union of some subfamily of $\mathcal B$. For any $x \in \mathcal V_1 \cap \mathcal V_2$, there is $B^1_x \in \mathcal V_1$ such that $x \in B^1_x$, and there is $B^1_x \in \mathcal V_2$ such that $x \in B^2_x$. By (2), there exists $B^3_x \in \mathcal B$ such that $x \in B^3_x$ and $B^3_x \subseteq B^1_x \cap B^2_x \subseteq \mathcal V_1 \cap \mathcal V_2$. Consequently, $V_1 \cap V_2$ can be expressed as follows: $$
\mathcal V_1 \cap \mathcal V_2 = \bigcap_{x \in \mathcal V_1 \cap \mathcal V_2} B^3_x
$$ Since $V_1 \cap V_2$ is a union of elements from $\mathcal{B}$, we conclude that $V_1 \cap V_2 \in \tau$.
